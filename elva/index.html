01 â Product strategy The foundation

V1 is clean.
But clean isn't enough.

Elva's v1 product is well-structured. The architecture is sound â sidebar navigation, task cards, a relocation timeline. The information is all there. But the experience doesn't yet match what Elva promises: a guide at the most significant moment of someone's career.

Right now it feels like a to-do list. It should feel like a trusted companion. The gap between those two things is entirely a design problem â and it's completely solvable without changing a single line of backend logic.

"The product should feel as considered as the service Elva provides."

Every screen should feel like someone thought carefully about this moment.

Motion with meaning

Transitions, reveals, and state changes that guide rather than distract. The kind of motion that Revolut gets right â purposeful, not decorative.

Interaction design

Human-first hierarchy

People are not case numbers. The product should lead with the person â their name, their progress, their next step â not a generic task list.

Information architecture

Scalable by design

Every design decision becomes a token â not a one-off. A system that any engineer can extend without breaking the craft that's been built into it.

Design system
01

Show, don't list

Replace flat text with visual progress. The journey should be felt, not just read.

02

Calm complexity

Relocation is stressful. The UI should reduce anxiety, not mirror it.

03

Earn every moment

Micro-interactions and animations that appear only where they add meaning.

04

One language

A token-based system so every new screen inherits the craft automatically.

The timeline needs to feel like a journey

Right now it's a list with dots. With the right motion and visual hierarchy, it can feel like real forward momentum â something that makes the employee feel excited, not anxious.

Progress deserves celebration

"0 of 12 tasks" is cold. A visual progress arc that fills as steps complete â with a subtle animation â turns the same data into a moment of recognition and encouragement.

Tasks need context, not just labels

A task labelled "Biometric appointment" doesn't tell you why it matters or how long it takes. Surfacing that context in the card itself removes anxiety and builds trust in the process.
